\title{Amortized Custom eFPGA Layout Search via Reinforcement Learning: Zero-Shot Generalization Across Circuit Benchmarks}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Custom eFPGA fabrics embed reconfigurability directly in fixed silicon ---
enabling domain-specific acceleration, post-fabrication protocol updates,
and IP security on a single chip --- but every search-based layout method
to date restarts from scratch for each new netlist, repeating hundreds of
expensive VTR evaluations with no information transferred from prior runs.
We train a reinforcement-learning policy, conditioned on a
graph-neural-network encoding of the target netlist, to choose DSP/BRAM
placement and fabric aspect ratio across a pool of 11 circuits; the same
checkpoint then applies zero-shot to circuits never seen in training.
Under a controlled comparison against a genetic algorithm sharing the
same oracle, metric, and baseline, a zero-shot rollout delivers an
immediate positive result in a single oracle call while the GA requires
768 VTR evaluations and ${\sim}$6.6 hours --- 50 of those generations
spent stagnating after its best was already found.
Trained jointly, the policy achieves 27.35\% aggregate in-pool ADP
reduction (three seeds); applied zero-shot to six held-out circuits ---
including fabrics up to $3.4\times$ the training area --- every result
is positive, the average is 31.23\%, and seed variance is markedly lower
than in-pool fitting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Embedding a small reconfigurable fabric in an ASIC or SoC --- an eFPGA ---
lets a fixed design adapt after fabrication: domain-specific acceleration,
post-fabrication protocol updates, IP security, and extended product life
can all be managed through the bitstream rather than a new chip
revision~\cite{golds,alice}. A generic FPGA is designed for arbitrary
workloads and is therefore over-provisioned for any single application ---
extra routing, DSP columns, and BRAM arrays that the deployed netlist never
fully uses. A custom eFPGA fabric, sized and arranged for one specific
netlist rather than general-purpose reconfigurability, can recover much of
that overhead. The product of routing area, critical-path delay, and dynamic power ---
which we abbreviate \textbf{ADP} throughout --- quantifies this overhead
directly: it measures how much the eFPGA fabric costs relative to hardening
the same function in standard cells, and minimizing it benefits every eFPGA
application~\cite{golds,nsgaii}. Note that GOLDS~\cite{golds} uses the same
abbreviation for the two-term Area$\times$Delay product; in
Section~\ref{sec:results-golds} we recompute results on their exact two-term
metric to make the comparison direct.

Two prior papers establish that per-instance search over DSP/BRAM placement
(and, in one case, CLB/arithmetic-block sizing) can substantially reduce the
eFPGA fabric overhead: GOLDS~\cite{golds} uses a genetic algorithm with an
Area$\times$Delay fitness function and reports up to $\sim$35\%
Area$\times$Delay improvement on the \texttt{diffeq1} benchmark; a follow-up applies NSGA-II to a larger,
multi-objective search space that additionally co-evolves CLB LUT size and
custom arithmetic-block sizing~\cite{nsgaii}. Both, however, are \emph{testbench-specific}: the genetic algorithm's
population is re-initialized and re-evolved from scratch for every new
target design, and every generation of every run requires its own full,
expensive VTR evaluation. Nothing learned while optimizing one netlist's
layout carries over to the next netlist — each new target benchmark pays the
full search cost again, with no way to amortize the work already done on
previous designs.

We train a single RL policy, conditioned on a GNN encoding of the target
netlist, to choose DSP/BRAM placement and fabric aspect ratio for a custom
eFPGA fabric — the problem where per-instance GA search has been the only
option. Unlike RL methods that assign blocks to an already-fabricated grid,
our policy \emph{constructs} the custom fabric layout itself. Trained jointly
across 11 circuits and scored by the real VTR flow at every step, the same
checkpoint then applies zero-shot to circuits never seen in training, including
fabrics up to $3.4\times$ the area of anything in the training pool.
Training runs once; each new netlist costs one
rollout. We quantify the search-cost gap against a from-scratch genetic algorithm
under an identical oracle, metric, and baseline: zero-shot, the policy
delivers an immediate positive result in one call; fine-tuned, it matches
the GA's final quality while the GA spends the majority of its 768
evaluations stagnating after its best was found early
(Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).

Concretely, this paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A GNN-conditioned RL policy for custom eFPGA DSP/BRAM placement and
        aspect-ratio selection. A fixed-size network encodes each target
        netlist so the same checkpoint applies to circuits never seen during
        training, evaluated directly against the VTR flow rather than a proxy
        (Sections~\ref{sec:gnn},~\ref{sec:universe}).
  \item A controlled cost comparison against a from-scratch genetic algorithm
        under an identical evaluation oracle, ADP metric, and baseline, on the
        held-out \texttt{softmax} benchmark: zero-shot, the policy delivers an
        immediate positive result in one oracle call; fine-tuned, it matches
        the GA's final ADP reduction while the GA expends 768 evaluations
        (${\sim}$6.6\,h) --- stagnating for the final 50 generations after
        its best individual was found at generation~46
        (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).
  \item A joint 11-benchmark training result (27.35\% aggregate in-pool ADP
        reduction, averaged over three seeds) (Section~\ref{sec:results-inpool}),
        and a literature-facing comparison against GOLDS' published metric and
        baseline, recomputed on a zero-shot evaluation: 37.4\% against GOLDS'
        $\sim$35\% (Section~\ref{sec:results-golds}).
  \item A three-seed reliability analysis exposing an asymmetry: in-pool
        fitting is far more seed-sensitive (two benchmarks spanning
        $\sim$28 points across seeds) than zero-shot generalization
        (at most $\sim$12 points across all six held-out benchmarks),
        reported as raw per-seed results rather than mean $\pm$ SD
        (Sections~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot},~\ref{sec:results-tradeoff}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Custom eFPGA Layout Design}

A generic island-style FPGA provisions DSP columns, BRAM columns, and routing
for arbitrary workloads; any single application leaves much of this capacity
unused, paying area and delay overhead for resources it never exercises. This
has motivated a line of work on \emph{custom} eFPGA layout: tailoring tile
placement and fabric dimensions to a specific target netlist so the deployed
fabric is compact and efficient rather than over-provisioned. Applications driving
this work include IP security (where the function implemented on the eFPGA is
absent from fixed silicon until configured~\cite{alice,arianna}),
domain-specific SoC acceleration, and post-fabrication
reconfigurability~\cite{golds}. Subsequent work reduces eFPGA overhead further
by shrinking under-utilized routing~\cite{shell} or de-regularizing the fabric
itself~\cite{nuredact} — both motivating the custom, non-island-style layouts
this paper synthesizes. All rely on heuristic or evolutionary search re-run
per design; none amortizes layout effort across netlists.
GOLDS~\cite{golds} introduced custom-tailored eFPGA layout search: a genetic
algorithm over DSP/BRAM tile placement on a fixed-size fabric, with an
Area$\times$Delay fitness evaluated through the real VTR flow, reporting up to
$\sim$35\% Area$\times$Delay improvement over a traditional island-style layout on
\texttt{diffeq1}. A follow-up~\cite{nsgaii} extends this to a multi-objective
NSGA-II search that additionally co-evolves CLB LUT size and custom DSP/BRAM
block sizing --- not just placement --- optimizing delay and power jointly and
reporting up to $\sim$44.6\% improvement on a larger design space. Both are
strong results; both start from a random population for every new target
design, transferring no information from prior runs. We target the same problem
GOLDS defines and use the same VTR oracle, which gives us a clean controlled
comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}); the NSGA-II co-evolution of block
sizing is a natural extension for future work (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\subsection{Learned Policies for Physical Design}

Reinforcement learning has been applied to chip-scale physical design.
AlphaChip~\cite{alphachip,alphachip_addendum} trains an RL agent to place ASIC
macro blocks using a learned reward proxy, and reports policies that transfer
across chip designs without retraining — the amortization argument we make
here, at a much larger design scale. Subsequent work explores visual state
representations~\cite{maskplace}, joint placement-and-routing~\cite{deeppr},
and offline RL over a transformer policy~\cite{chipformer}. All motivate the
claim this paper tests in a smaller-scale setting: a trained policy amortizes
layout search cost across designs in a way that per-instance search cannot. Unlike
AlphaChip, we evaluate every candidate against the true VTR flow rather than a
learned proxy, trading speed for fidelity — affordable at our scale, where a
VTR run completes in seconds to low minutes (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}).

Within the FPGA setting specifically, RL has guided placement onto an
\emph{already-fabricated} grid — selecting annealing perturbations in
VPR~\cite{rlplace} or driving a Gym-style VPR environment~\cite{vprgym} — but
always assigns blocks to a fixed grid, never shapes the grid itself. Transferable
EDA policies have appeared for tool tuning~\cite{fasttuner} and global
placement~\cite{transplace}. Our policy's role is different: it synthesizes the
custom FPGA fabric geometry rather than placing onto an existing one.

\subsection{VTR/VPR Background}

We build on the open-source Verilog-to-Routing (VTR)~\cite{vtr9,vtr8,vpr97}
toolchain: Yosys performs synthesis (via the VTR integration~\cite{parmys})
and VPR performs packing, placement, and
routing against an architecture described in an XML schema of tile types,
positions, and connectivity. This architecture XML is the artifact GOLDS and
the NSGA-II follow-up generate by hand-coded search and that we generate by
policy rollout (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}); VTR's acceptance of an arbitrary
heterogeneous tile layout, not only the regular island-style grids it ships,
is what makes this entire line of work possible.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

A clarification on scope. The reconfigurability in this setting is not
general-purpose: a fabric optimized for one netlist will not accommodate a
circuit with substantially different DSP/BRAM requirements. This is
intentional --- the tradeoff is reduced cross-circuit generality in exchange
for a significantly smaller, more efficient implementation. Reconfigurability
means updating the same primary function (parameter changes, post-fabrication
fixes, a known set of operational modes), not repurposing the fabric for an
unrelated workload. Optimizing for one target netlist is the design goal.

We separate two roles that are easily conflated. The \emph{policy} designs
the fabric; the \emph{VTR toolchain} maps the circuit onto it. Concretely, given a
netlist requiring $n_{\mathrm{dsp}}$ DSP and $n_{\mathrm{bram}}$ BRAM hard
blocks, the policy emits an architecture descriptor
$\pi = (\mathrm{ar}, \{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n_{\mathrm{dsp}}+n_{\mathrm{bram}}})$:
a fabric aspect ratio $\mathrm{ar} \in \mathcal{R}$ and a tile coordinate for
every hard block. VTR then runs the full synthesis-to-routing flow on that
architecture --- it synthesizes the Verilog, packs the remaining logic into CLB
clusters, places every block (CLBs included), routes, and auto-sizes the array
to the smallest grid of the chosen aspect ratio that holds the design. The
policy thus never places a CLB or fixes the grid dimensions: it shapes the
fabric and positions the hard blocks, and VTR reports back the realized cost.
We take that cost to be the product of routing area, critical-path delay, and
dynamic power (our three-term ADP metric, as defined in Section~\ref{sec:intro}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:adp}
  \mathrm{ADP}(\pi) = \mathrm{Area}(\pi) \times \mathrm{Delay}(\pi) \times \mathrm{Power}(\pi),
\end{equation}
where each term is reported by the VTR (Yosys + VPR) flow
(Section~\ref{sec:setup}). We use VPR's \emph{routing area} --- the physical tile-grid footprint
including routing channels --- rather than a logical area measure such as CLB
count. The policy controls fabric geometry (aspect ratio, tile positions),
which changes routing area; logical CLB area is determined by the netlist and
is approximately layout-independent. Routing area is therefore the metric that
responds to policy decisions and reflects the actual on-chip die area of the
deployed eFPGA. We report results
as percentage ADP reduction relative to a traditional, non-customized
island-style eFPGA baseline at the same logic capacity.

\subsection{RL Formulation}
\label{sec:rl}

We pose placement as a finite-horizon sequential decision process and train a
policy with Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), using the maskable
variant~\cite{maskableppo} (\texttt{sb3\_contrib}'s \texttt{MaskablePPO}) so
that the action space can be
restricted to currently-legal tile coordinates at every step.

\textbf{Episode structure.} An episode lays out exactly one benchmark's hard
blocks on the shared canvas. Step 0 selects the fabric aspect ratio from a fixed
discrete set $\mathcal{R}$; each subsequent step positions one hard block ---
BRAMs first, then DSPs, each group ordered by descending shared-net
connectivity, so the most-connected blocks are committed earliest. Once all
hard blocks are positioned, the environment bakes the layout into an
architecture XML and a placement-constraints file (via Jinja2 templating) and
invokes the VTR flow to obtain $\mathrm{Area}(\pi)$, $\mathrm{Delay}(\pi)$, and
$\mathrm{Power}(\pi)$; all intermediate steps receive zero reward.

\textbf{Action space.} The action space is $\mathrm{Discrete}(W_{\max} \times
H_{\max})$, fixed across all benchmarks in a training run (Section~\ref{sec:universe}).
At step 0 an action index selects an entry in $\mathcal{R}$; at later steps an
action index decodes to a tile coordinate $(x,y)$ via a fixed stride on
$H_{\max}$, independent of which benchmark is active. Action masking — keyed
off the \emph{active} benchmark's real width/height and the blocks already
placed — restricts the policy to legal, non-overlapping, in-bounds coordinates
for that specific benchmark and step.

\textbf{Reward.} The terminal reward is a weighted sum of negative log-ratios
against the traditional baseline for the active benchmark:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
  r = -\Big(
    w_a \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Area}}{\mathrm{Area}_0} +
    w_d \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Delay}}{\mathrm{Delay}_0} +
    w_p \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Power}}{\mathrm{Power}_0}
  \Big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Area}_0,\mathrm{Delay}_0,\mathrm{Power}_0$ are the
traditional-baseline metrics for that benchmark and $w_a,w_d,w_p$ are
configurable weights (Section~\ref{sec:training}). With $w_a=w_d=w_p=1$,
$r$ equals $\log(\mathrm{ADP}_0/\mathrm{ADP})$ — i.e.\ the reward
\emph{is} the log of the ADP-improvement ratio in Eq.~\ref{eq:adp}, making
reward and our headline metric directly comparable. An invalid choice
(degenerate aspect ratio, out-of-bounds or overlapping placement, or a VTR
flow failure) terminates the episode early with a fixed penalty reward of $-10$.

\subsection{GCN Feature Extractor}
\label{sec:gnn}

To let a single policy generalize across benchmarks with different netlist
sizes, hard-block counts, and grid dimensions, we encode each netlist as a
graph rather than a flat vector, giving the policy the inductive bias to
transfer across circuit topologies that graph learning provides
elsewhere in EDA~\cite{gamora}. We first reduce each benchmark's full netlist
graph to a compact, fixed-schema representation: one
node per DSP/BRAM hard-block instance, plus one aggregate ``fabric'' node
summarizing the remaining standard-cell logic. Each node carries a 4-dimensional
feature vector \texttt{[is\_dsp, is\_bram, is\_fabric, net\_count\_normalized]}.
Edges between two hard-block nodes are weighted by their raw shared-net count;
edges between a hard-block node and the fabric node are weighted by the summed
shared-net count across the standard cells that block touches. As these two
edge kinds live on very different scales, we normalize each into $[0,1]$
separately by a log compression $w_{\mathrm{norm}} = \log(1+w)/\log(1+c)$ with
a per-kind ceiling $c$ from the training pool, so neither type saturates or
vanishes.

This reduced graph is padded to a fixed $(\mathrm{MAX\_NODES},
\mathrm{MAX\_EDGES})$ — computed once over the full benchmark \emph{universe}
(Section~\ref{sec:universe}) — and fed to a small GNN feature extractor shared
by the policy and value heads: two \texttt{GCNConv} layers (hidden width 64)
produce per-node embeddings $h_i$; a global mean pool gives a whole-netlist
embedding, and the embedding of the node being placed this step gives a
focused per-step embedding. These are concatenated with a small CNN over the
occupancy grid (4 channels: occupancy, block-type hint, aspect ratio, net
count) and the active benchmark's normalized $(\mathrm{width},
\mathrm{height})$, then projected to a 128-dimensional vector for the PPO
policy and value heads. The extractor is trained end-to-end under the PPO
loss, with no separate GNN pretraining.

\subsection{Universe Superset and Action Masking}
\label{sec:universe}

A policy's input/output dimensions are fixed at construction, yet we want one
checkpoint loadable against benchmarks never trained on, including ones
discovered later. We therefore size every dimension-dependent quantity —
canvas $(W_{\max}, H_{\max})$, $\mathrm{MAX\_NODES}$, $\mathrm{MAX\_EDGES}$ —
from a benchmark \emph{universe} that is a deliberate superset of the sampled
training set, including benchmarks reserved purely for zero-shot evaluation,
provided none exceeds the shared dimensions. Per-episode action masking
(Section~\ref{sec:rl}) and the \texttt{valid\_wh} observation let the same
fixed-size network operate on each benchmark's smaller real footprint within
that canvas.

\subsection{Training Setup}
\label{sec:training}

Each training run samples uniformly across the 11-benchmark training pool
(Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar}) episode-by-episode, so a single policy is
updated jointly on all 11 circuits rather than one at a time. We use
\texttt{MaskablePPO} with 24 parallel environments, 48 rollout steps per
environment per update, batch size 144, 15 epochs per update, entropy
coefficient 0.01, and reward weights $w_a=w_d=w_p=1$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}),
giving reward $=\log(\mathrm{ADP}_0/\mathrm{ADP})$. Each run trains for
13{,}000 episodes (capped independently of total timesteps) with a
per-episode VTR timeout of 300s to keep a degenerate early-exploration
episode from stalling the other 23 parallel workers. We report results from
three independent training seeds (7, 42, 123) under this identical
configuration, used throughout Section~\ref{sec:results} to characterize
result reliability rather than report a single run's numbers as ground
truth.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Evaluation Oracle}
\label{sec:oracle}

Every candidate placement is scored by the true downstream toolchain, not a
proxy. A placement (aspect ratio + hard-block coordinates) is rendered into a
complete VTR architecture XML and a VPR placement-constraints file via Jinja2
templates parameterized by tile coordinates, block type, and fabric aspect
ratio. This architecture and the benchmark's Verilog are then run through the
full VTR flow — Yosys-based synthesis, VPR packing, placement, and routing,
followed by VPR's power estimator against a 45nm PTM technology file — and we
extract routing area, critical-path delay, and dynamic power from
the resulting VPR reports. All evaluations use a VTR 9.0.0 development build
(git \texttt{22a09d39}, GCC 13.3.0). Evaluated (placement, benchmark) pairs
are cached, since the synthesis-to-routing flow is the dominant cost of both
training and evaluation (seconds to low minutes per call depending on
benchmark size).

\subsection{Baseline}
\label{sec:baseline}

Our traditional baseline is VTR's default island-style architecture template
(\texttt{k6\_frac\_N10\_mem32K\_40nm}: 6-input LUTs, cluster size 10, 32K-bit
BRAMs, 40nm) at matched logic capacity for each benchmark — the same template
used by GOLDS~\cite{golds} as its traditional-layout comparison point, which
lets us compare percentage improvements directly against theirs on a shared
starting point (Section~\ref{sec:results-golds}).

\subsection{Benchmarks}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} lists all 17 benchmarks with their grid size and hard-block counts. The pool comprises an
11-benchmark training set of real circuits spanning a range of DSP/BRAM
resource profiles, and a held-out set of six circuits used only for zero-shot
evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}), never sampled during training.
Training circuits come from the standard VTR suite~\cite{vtr8}; three held-out
benchmarks (\texttt{softmax}, \texttt{reduction\_layer}, \texttt{arm\_core})
together with \texttt{mkDelayWorker32B} are larger standard circuits (from
Koios~\cite{koios} and the VTR suite) that also probe fabric sizes well beyond
the training pool. Because the standard suites contain few \emph{small}
heterogeneous designs, we add two small probes —
\texttt{lightweight\_cipher} and \texttt{custom\_macbuf}, the latter an
internally built multiply-accumulate buffer — to retain coverage at the
small-fabric end of the held-out set. The GA head-to-head
(Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}) uses \texttt{softmax}, a standard Koios
benchmark held out of RL training.


\subsection{Baselines Compared}
\label{sec:baselines-compared}

We compare against: (1) the traditional island-style eFPGA baseline
(Section~\ref{sec:baseline}); (2) a genetic algorithm we implemented under the
same evaluation oracle and ADP metric (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}); and
(3) published results from GOLDS~\cite{golds} and the NSGA-II
follow-up~\cite{nsgaii}, subject to the metric caveat in
Section~\ref{sec:results-golds}.

\subsection{In-Pool Performance}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} (upper group) reports per-benchmark in-pool ADP
reduction for the 11-benchmark policy on its own training set across three
independent seeds (7, 42, 123; Section~\ref{sec:training}). We show all three
per-seed outcomes rather than a mean $\pm$ SD, which three runs cannot
justify (Section~\ref{sec:results-tradeoff}). Each seed's pooled aggregate
— one minus the ratio of summed ADP across all 11 benchmarks to summed
baseline ADP, \emph{not} a simple mean of per-benchmark percentages — is
35.73\%, 23.11\%, and 23.97\% (mean 27.35\%).
\texttt{raygentop} is consistently negative across all three seeds ($[-2.2, -1.7]\%$).
Its 6-DSP/1-BRAM profile is the only one in the pool combining a large DSP
count with a BRAM (other DSP-heavy benchmarks have no BRAM; other
BRAM-having ones have $\le1$ DSP), and despite a fair $\approx$9.1\% episode
share the policy finds no improving placement for this configuration —
plausibly a representational gap rather than a convergence shortfall, and our
most immediate diagnostic target. Two benchmarks, \texttt{or1200}
and \texttt{boundtop}, account for almost all of the seed-to-seed
instability: their per-seed outcomes span $5.8$--$33.5\%$ and
$21.3$--$50.7\%$ respectively, ranges three to four times wider than any
other benchmark in the pool (all $\le8$ points). We analyze this
asymmetry in Section~\ref{sec:results-tradeoff}.

\subsection{GA Head-to-Head Comparisons}
\label{sec:results-ga}

To establish a controlled cost comparison — without the metric-conversion
caveat of Section~\ref{sec:results-golds} — we implemented a GA under the
same oracle, ADP metric, and baseline as the RL pipeline and ran it on
\texttt{softmax}: roulette-wheel selection, single-point crossover, and
per-gene mutation over hard-block coordinates and fabric aspect ratio,
population~8 (matching the eight parallel RL workers), capped at 500
generations with patience~50.

Table~\ref{tab:ga} reports the outcome. The GA's best individual ---
\textbf{\GAred\%} ADP reduction, comparable to the fine-tuned policy's
\textbf{14.82\%} --- appeared at generation~46, after 368 VTR evaluations
and ${\sim}$2.75\,h of wall-clock. The GA then ran for 50 more generations
(400 additional evaluations, ${\sim}$3.8\,h) without improvement before the
patience condition terminated it, for a total of \GAcalls~calls and
\GAhours. Zero-shot, the policy achieves \textbf{5.39\%} ADP reduction in a
\emph{single} oracle call (${\sim}$134\,s); fine-tuned, it matches the GA's
quality at ${\sim}$episode~552 (${\sim}$4\,h) and continues to 14.82\% at
episode~1{,}128 (${\sim}$8\,h).

The timing exposes a structural difference. The GA's best appeared at
generation~46 and never improved; the remaining 50 generations were spent on
a stagnated population that could not escape the local optimum. Per-generation
averages confirm this: the typical individual is worse than baseline in
\emph{every} generation --- the GA carries forward a lucky draw but never
updates its sampling distribution. Gradient updates do exactly this for the
RL policy: the fraction of episodes beating baseline rises from ${\sim}$10\%
early in fine-tuning to ${\sim}$75\% by the end, which is why the policy
continues improving past the point where the GA stalled. The GA's
evaluation cost is re-incurred from scratch for every new target design;
the policy's training cost is fixed, so the amortization advantage grows
with the number of designs a team must handle.

\subsection{Head-to-Head Against GOLDS}
\label{sec:results-golds}

As a secondary, literature-facing comparison, we recompute a genuine
zero-shot result --- a policy trained only on \texttt{diffeq2}, evaluated
zero-shot on \texttt{diffeq1} with no benchmark-specific training --- using
GOLDS'~\cite{golds} exact two-term Area$\times$Delay metric and the same
traditional baseline architecture they report against. Two differences from
the rest of this paper apply: (1) we substitute the two-term
Area$\times$Delay product for our three-term Area$\times$Delay$\times$Power
metric, and (2) we note that GOLDS' area term is a logical measure of fabric
utilization while ours (Section~\ref{sec:problem}) is VPR's routing area; to
match GOLDS as closely as possible we recompute on their metric and baseline
rather than ours. This yields a \textbf{37.4\%} reduction versus GOLDS'
published best-of-population result of \textbf{$\sim$35\%} on the same
benchmark --- a real, if narrower, margin, achieved with zero benchmark-specific
training. Nowhere else in this paper do we compare two-term numbers against
our three-term results directly.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Generalization}
\label{sec:results-zeroshot}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} (lower group) reports ADP reduction on all six
held-out benchmarks (Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar}), across the same three seeds
as Section~\ref{sec:results-inpool}, evaluated with zero benchmark-specific
training. Two of the six (\texttt{softmax}, \texttt{reduction\_layer}) were
part of the training-time \emph{universe} used to size the policy's tensors
(Section~\ref{sec:universe}) but never sampled during training; the other
four were entirely outside the universe as well, a strictly harder
generalization test. Crucially, four of the six are fabrics
\emph{larger than anything in the training pool} (up to $2{,}304$ tiles versus
the pool's $676$), so this is size extrapolation, not
interpolation. Every benchmark finishes positive under every seed —
no sign flips at all, unlike the in-pool result — and every per-seed range is
narrow (at most $\approx12$ points, for \texttt{custom\_macbuf}), with no
benchmark approaching the \texttt{or1200}/\texttt{boundtop} spread.
The overall average across all six benchmarks and three seeds is
\textbf{31.23\%}. The weakest benchmarks (\texttt{softmax}, \texttt{arm\_core})
show proportionally larger seed spread relative to their mean, but the
absolute range remains small (at most $\approx12$ points) in every case.

All reported numbers use greedy (deterministic) decoding, which is the
intended deployment mode. As a diagnostic, stochastic rollouts on one seed
yielded negative average ADP reductions on 4 of 6 benchmarks, confirming
that the learned distribution is concentrated: the greedy action occupies a
high-quality region while most of the probability mass does not. The zero-shot
results should be read as the top-choice performance per seed, not an
expectation over sampling.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning Efficiency}
\label{sec:results-finetune}

We also characterize continued learning on a partially-known benchmark
(\texttt{diffeq1}, in the training pool): fine-tuning the multi-benchmark
policy versus training from scratch, both 6{,}000 episodes under identical
hyperparameters and separate caches. The fine-tuned run dominates typical
reward throughout (0.785 vs.\ 0.736 in the final window) and plateaus by
$\approx$episode 3{,}000, while scratch never plateaus within its budget. The
mechanism is entropy collapse: the fine-tuned policy's entropy falls from 2.00
to 0.79 (vs.\ scratch's 4.63 to 2.42), so only 31.5\% of its episodes are
novel VTR evaluations versus 94.4\% for scratch — a sample-efficiency win
that, as Section~\ref{sec:results-tradeoff} notes, also caps further
exploration beyond the pretrained prior. This is continued learning, not
cross-benchmark transfer (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}).

\subsection{Seed Variance: In-Pool vs.\ Zero-Shot}
\label{sec:results-tradeoff}

Across three independent training seeds, the 11-benchmark policy exhibits a
clear asymmetry: \emph{in-pool} performance is considerably more
seed-sensitive than \emph{zero-shot} performance. For nine of the eleven
training benchmarks, the per-seed range is small ($\le8$ points); two
benchmarks, \texttt{or1200} and \texttt{boundtop}, span $\sim$28 and
$\sim$29 points respectively — three to four times the spread of any other
benchmark — and account for almost the entire spread in the pooled aggregate
(35.73\% / 23.11\% / 23.97\% across seeds). On the six held-out zero-shot
benchmarks, by contrast, every per-seed range stays within $\sim$12 points
(Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar}); the two shaded in-pool benchmarks have the widest
ranges in either group.

We do not have a confirmed mechanism but propose one. In-pool performance
depends on which benchmarks a given seed converges on slowly within the shared
episode budget, whereas zero-shot performance reflects a more general property
of the learned representation (Section~\ref{sec:gnn}), independent of any one
benchmark's convergence. The entropy collapse of
Section~\ref{sec:results-finetune} compounds this: a seed converging early on
most benchmarks retains fewer episodes for a slow-to-converge benchmark such as \texttt{or1200}.
No seed leads on both high-variance benchmarks and elsewhere at once
(seed 42 leads on \texttt{or1200} and \texttt{boundtop}; seeds 7 and 123 lead
on others), pointing to per-benchmark convergence variance rather than a
globally better seed. At $n=3$ this is a characterization; more seeds would
establish whether the asymmetry is stable.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A single policy trained across 11 circuits answers the cost question directly:
under a controlled comparison on an identical oracle, ADP metric, and baseline,
the GA stagnates after generation~46 and spends \GAcalls\ total evaluations
and \GAhours\ finding nothing beyond its early best. The fine-tuned policy
matches that quality and keeps improving; zero-shot, the policy delivers an
immediate positive result in one call (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).
The same policy generalizes zero-shot to six held-out benchmarks --- four of
them fabrics larger than anything seen in training ---
and, recomputed on GOLDS' published metric, exceeds their reported result
(Sections~\ref{sec:results-golds},~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}).

Two reliability findings qualify these results. In-pool fitting is far more
seed-sensitive than zero-shot transfer (aggregate 23.11--35.73\% across seeds,
driven by \texttt{or1200} and \texttt{boundtop}), and deterministic zero-shot
numbers diverge from stochastic sampling (negative for 4 of 6 benchmarks).
A single-seed or single-rollout number is not a stable characterization
(Section~\ref{sec:results-tradeoff}).

Three gaps are worth naming: results rest on three seeds and a systematic
stochastic-rollout evaluation remains outstanding; the action space covers
placement and aspect ratio only, where co-evolving CLB LUT size and
arithmetic-block sizing~\cite{nsgaii} is a natural GCN extension; and
broadening to the full GOLDS/NSGA-II benchmark set would allow direct
comparisons without the metric conversion of Section~\ref{sec:results-golds}.
