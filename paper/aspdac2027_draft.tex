\title{Amortized eFPGA Layout Search via Reinforcement Learning: Zero-Shot Generalization Across Circuit Benchmarks}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
eFPGA fabrics embed reconfigurability in fixed silicon for
acceleration, post-fabrication updates, and IP security. We train a
single graph-and-grid-conditioned RL policy that places H-blocks (DSP/BRAM) and
selects the fabric aspect ratio across 11 circuits, then apply the same
checkpoint zero-shot to circuits never seen in training. Jointly trained,
the policy reaches 27.60\% aggregate in-pool ADP reduction (three seeds);
applied zero-shot to six held-out circuits (including fabrics up to
$3.4\times$ the training area), every result is positive, averaging
31.23\%, with markedly lower seed variance than in-pool. Against a GA
sharing the same VTR oracle, metric, and baseline, the fine-tuned policy
reaches a comparable best ADP reduction, while zero-shot already lands a
positive result with no per-design search at all. Unlike per-instance
search, the trained policy amortizes: each new netlist costs one rollout,
not a restarted search.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Embedding a small reconfigurable fabric into otherwise fixed silicon, an
eFPGA, lets a chip adapt after fabrication, whether as a standalone
ASIC or as an accelerator block inside a larger SoC: acceleration, protocol
updates, IP security, and extended product life managed through the
bitstream rather than a chip respin~\cite{golds,alice}.
A generic FPGA is over-provisioned for any single application: it contains
extra routing and heterogeneous blocks (\emph{H-blocks}: DSP slices, BRAMs)
the deployed netlist never fully uses.
An eFPGA fabric sized for one specific netlist can recover much of
that overhead. The product of routing area, critical-path delay, and dynamic
power (\textbf{ADP} throughout) quantifies this overhead directly, and
minimizing it benefits every eFPGA application~\cite{golds,nsgaii}.\footnote{GOLDS~\cite{golds} uses the same abbreviation, ADP, for a different, two-term Area$\times$Delay product; ours is three-term (Area$\times$Delay$\times$Power) throughout this paper.}

Two prior papers show per-instance search substantially reduces eFPGA overhead:
GOLDS~\cite{golds} uses a GA with Area$\times$Delay fitness; a follow-up
applies NSGA-II and also co-evolves CLB LUT size and arithmetic-block
sizing~\cite{nsgaii}. Both report gains on their own
Area$\times$Delay metric (Section~\ref{sec:related}), but both are
\emph{testbench-specific}: each run re-initializes from scratch,
and nothing learned on one netlist transfers to the next:
every new target pays the full search cost again.

We train a single graph-and-grid-conditioned RL policy to choose H-block placement
and fabric aspect ratio, the problem where per-instance GA search has
been the only option. Unlike RL methods that assign blocks to an
already-fabricated grid, our policy \emph{constructs} the fabric
layout itself. Trained jointly on 11 circuits and scored by the real VTR
flow, the same checkpoint applies zero-shot to unseen circuits, including
fabrics up to $3.4\times$ the training pool area.
Training runs once; each new netlist costs one rollout.
We compare against a from-scratch GA under identical oracle, metric, and
baseline: the fine-tuned policy reaches a comparable best ADP reduction,
while zero-shot already gives a positive result with no per-design search
at all (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).

This paper contributes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A graph-and-grid-conditioned RL policy for H-block placement and
        aspect-ratio selection, with one fixed-size checkpoint applied
        zero-shot to unseen circuits and evaluated against the true VTR
        flow rather than a proxy (Sections~\ref{sec:gnn},~\ref{sec:capacity}).
  \item A comparison against a GA on held-out \texttt{softmax} under the
        same oracle, metric, and baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).
  \item An aggregate in-pool ADP reduction of 27.60\% across three seeds
        (Section~\ref{sec:results-inpool}).
  \item A three-seed reliability analysis comparing in-pool and zero-shot
        seed sensitivity, reported as raw per-seed results
        (Sections~\ref{sec:results-inpool},~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Per-Design eFPGA Layout}

A generic island-style FPGA provisions H-blocks (DSP, BRAM) and routing for arbitrary
workloads; any single application leaves much of this unused, paying area
and delay for resources it never exercises. This motivates fitting tile
placement and fabric dimensions to one specific netlist for a compact,
efficient fabric. Applications include IP security
(function absent from fixed silicon until configured~\cite{alice,arianna}),
domain-specific acceleration, and post-fabrication reconfigurability~\cite{golds}.
Subsequent work reduces overhead by shrinking under-utilized routing~\cite{shell}
or de-regularizing the fabric~\cite{nuredact}, both motivating the
non-island-style layouts this paper synthesizes. All rely on per-design
search; none amortizes layout effort across netlists.
GOLDS~\cite{golds} introduced layout search built for a single design: a GA over
H-block placement with Area$\times$Delay fitness through the real VTR flow.
A follow-up~\cite{nsgaii} applies NSGA-II, also co-evolving CLB LUT size
and H-block sizing. Both report per-design gains on their own two-term
Area$\times$Delay metric, a different quantity from the three-term ADP
this paper reports. Both start from a random
population for every new design, transferring
nothing from prior runs. We target the same problem GOLDS defines with the
same VTR oracle, enabling a clean controlled comparison
(Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}); block-sizing co-evolution is a natural
extension (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\subsection{Learned Policies for Physical Design}

AlphaChip~\cite{alphachip,alphachip_addendum} trains an RL agent to place
ASIC macros using a learned proxy and reports cross-design transfer:
the amortization argument we make here, at a much larger scale.
Subsequent work explores visual representations~\cite{maskplace}, joint
placement-and-routing~\cite{deeppr}, and transformer policies~\cite{chipformer},
all supporting the claim that a trained policy amortizes layout cost across
designs as per-instance search cannot. Unlike AlphaChip, we evaluate every
candidate against the true VTR flow, not a proxy: affordable at our
scale, where a VTR run takes seconds to low minutes (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}).

Within the FPGA setting, RL has guided placement onto an
\emph{already-fabricated} grid (annealing perturbations in
VPR~\cite{rlplace} or a Gym-style VPR environment~\cite{vprgym}), but
always assigns blocks to a fixed grid, never shapes it. Transferable EDA
policies exist for tool tuning~\cite{fasttuner} and global
placement~\cite{transplace}; our policy instead synthesizes the
fabric geometry.

\subsection{VTR/VPR Background}

We build on the open-source Verilog-to-Routing (VTR)~\cite{vtr9}:
Yosys synthesizes the design~\cite{parmys} and VPR~\cite{vpr97} packs, places, and routes
against an architecture XML of tile types, positions, and connectivity. This
XML (which GOLDS and NSGA-II generate by search and we generate by policy
rollout) must represent arbitrary heterogeneous layouts, not just
island-style grids, which is what makes this entire line of work possible.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

A fabric optimized for one netlist trades general-purpose flexibility for
a significantly smaller, more efficient implementation: it accommodates
parameter changes, post-fabrication fixes, and a known set of operational
modes on that one primary function, not arbitrary workloads with
substantially different H-block requirements.

Two roles are easily conflated: the \emph{policy} designs the fabric; the
\emph{VTR toolchain} maps the circuit onto it. Given a netlist requiring
$n_{\mathrm{dsp}}$ DSP and $n_{\mathrm{bram}}$ BRAM blocks, the policy emits
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:policy}
  \pi = \Big(\mathrm{ar},\ \{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n_{\mathrm{dsp}}+n_{\mathrm{bram}}}\Big):
\end{equation}
a fabric aspect ratio $\mathrm{ar} \in \mathcal{R}$ and a tile coordinate for
every H-block. VTR then synthesizes, packs, places, routes, and auto-sizes
the array to the smallest grid of the chosen aspect ratio that holds the design.
The policy never places a CLB or fixes grid dimensions; it shapes the fabric
and positions H-blocks; VTR reports back the realized cost.
We take that cost to be the three-term ADP (Section~\ref{sec:intro}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:adp}
  \mathrm{ADP}(\pi) = \mathrm{Area}(\pi) \times \mathrm{Delay}(\pi) \times \mathrm{Power}(\pi),
\end{equation}
where each term is from the VTR flow (Section~\ref{sec:setup}). Area is
VPR's \emph{routing area}: the physical tile-grid footprint including
routing channels, which tracks the fabric geometry the policy controls
and reflects actual on-chip die area; logical CLB area stays fixed
regardless of layout. We report results as percentage ADP reduction
relative to a traditional island-style baseline at matched logic capacity.

\subsection{RL Formulation}
\label{sec:rl}

We pose placement as a finite-horizon sequential decision process and train
with Maskable PPO~\cite{maskableppo} (\texttt{MaskablePPO}), which restricts
the action space to legal tile coordinates at each step.

\textbf{Episode structure.} Step 0 selects the fabric aspect ratio from
$\mathcal{R}$; each subsequent step positions one H-block (BRAMs then DSPs,
both ordered by descending shared-net connectivity). Once all blocks are placed,
the environment renders the layout into an architecture XML via Jinja2 and invokes
VTR to obtain $\mathrm{Area}(\pi)$, $\mathrm{Delay}(\pi)$, $\mathrm{Power}(\pi)$;
intermediate steps receive zero reward.

\textbf{Action space.} The action space is $\mathrm{Discrete}(W_{\max} \times
H_{\max})$, fixed across all benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:capacity}).
Step-0 actions select from $\mathcal{R}$; later steps decode an index to
$(x,y)$ via a fixed stride on $H_{\max}$. Action masking restricts the policy
to legal, non-overlapping, in-bounds coordinates for the active benchmark.

\textbf{Reward.} The terminal reward is a weighted sum of negative log-ratios
against the traditional baseline for the active benchmark:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
  r = -\Big(
    w_a \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Area}}{\mathrm{Area}_0} +
    w_d \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Delay}}{\mathrm{Delay}_0} +
    w_p \log\tfrac{\mathrm{Power}}{\mathrm{Power}_0}
  \Big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Area}_0,\mathrm{Delay}_0,\mathrm{Power}_0$ are the
traditional-baseline metrics and $w_a,w_d,w_p$ are configurable
(Section~\ref{sec:training}). With $w_a=w_d=w_p=1$,
$r = \log(\mathrm{ADP}_0/\mathrm{ADP})$, making reward and headline metric
directly comparable. An invalid choice (degenerate aspect ratio,
out-of-bounds or overlapping placement, or a VTR failure) terminates early
with penalty $-10$.

\subsection{GCN Feature Extractor}
\label{sec:gnn}

To generalize across benchmarks with varying netlist sizes, H-block counts,
and grid dimensions, we encode each netlist as a graph, giving the policy the
inductive bias for cross-topology transfer that graph learning provides
elsewhere in EDA~\cite{gamora}. We reduce each netlist to a compact
fixed-schema graph: one node per H-block instance plus one aggregate
``fabric'' node for the remaining standard-cell logic, with node features
\texttt{[is\_dsp, is\_bram, is\_fabric, net\_count\_normalized]}. Edges are
weighted by shared-net count, log-normalized to $[0,1]$, both between
H-blocks and between each H-block and the fabric node.

The graph is padded to a fixed maximum node and edge count
(Section~\ref{sec:capacity}) and encoded by two width-64 graph convolution
layers, giving every H-block a learned embedding. Global mean-pooling
yields a whole-netlist embedding, and the embedding of the node under
consideration at the current step yields a per-step embedding; both are
concatenated with a CNN encoding of the 4-channel occupancy grid and the
benchmark's normalized fabric dimensions, then projected to a single
128-dimensional vector consumed by the PPO actor and critic heads. The
encoder trains end-to-end under the PPO objective, with no separate
pretraining stage.

\subsection{Fixed-Capacity Sizing and Action Masking}
\label{sec:capacity}

Policy dimensions are fixed at construction, yet we want one checkpoint
loadable against benchmarks never trained on. We therefore size every
dimension-dependent quantity, the maximum fabric width and height and the
maximum node and edge count, from a deliberate superset of benchmark
sizes that includes ones reserved for zero-shot evaluation. Per-episode
action masking restricts the fixed-size network to each benchmark's
smaller real footprint.

\subsection{Training Setup}
\label{sec:training}

Training samples uniformly from the benchmark pool, episode-by-episode.
We use \texttt{MaskablePPO}
with 24 parallel environments, 48 rollout steps per update, batch size 144,
15 epochs per update, entropy coefficient 0.01, and reward weights
$w_a=w_d=w_p=1$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}), giving reward
$=\log(\mathrm{ADP}_0/\mathrm{ADP})$. Each run trains for 13{,}000 episodes
with a 300s VTR timeout per episode. We report three independent seeds (7, 42,
123) throughout Section~\ref{sec:results} to characterize reliability rather
than present a single run as ground truth.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Evaluation Oracle}
\label{sec:oracle}

Every candidate placement is scored by the true toolchain, not a proxy.
A placement is rendered into a VTR architecture XML and VPR constraints
file via Jinja2 templates, then run through the full VTR flow (synthesis,
packing, placement, routing, and power estimation against a 45nm PTM file),
and routing area, critical-path delay, and dynamic power are extracted
from VPR reports. All evaluations use VTR 9.0.0 (git \texttt{22a09d39},
GCC 13.3.0). Evaluated pairs are cached; the synthesis-to-routing flow
dominates cost (seconds to low minutes per call).

\subsection{Benchmarks}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} lists all 17 benchmarks with grid size and
H-block counts: 11 for training and 6 held-out for zero-shot evaluation
only (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}), drawn from the VTR suite~\cite{vtr8},
Koios~\cite{koios}, and two small probes authored for this work, covering the
small-fabric end the standard suites underrepresent. Four held-out
benchmarks (\texttt{softmax}, \texttt{reduction\_layer}, \texttt{arm\_core},
\texttt{mkDelayWorker32B}) are larger circuits, probing fabric sizes well
beyond the training pool. The GA comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga})
uses \texttt{softmax}, held out of RL training.


\subsection{Baselines Compared}
\label{sec:baselines-compared}

We compare against: (1) the traditional island-style eFPGA baseline\footnote{VTR's
default island-style template (\texttt{k6\_frac\_N10\_\allowbreak mem32K\_40nm}:
6-input LUTs, cluster size 10, 32K-bit BRAMs, 40nm) at matched logic
capacity, the same template GOLDS~\cite{golds} uses.}; and (2) a genetic
algorithm implemented under the same evaluation oracle and ADP metric
(Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}).


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{In-Pool Performance}
\label{sec:results-inpool}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.86\columnwidth]{fig_seed_variance.pdf}
\caption{Per-seed ADP reduction for all 17 benchmarks (11 in-pool, 6 held-out zero-shot).}
\label{fig:seedvar}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} (upper group) shows per-benchmark in-pool ADP
reduction across three seeds. The pooled aggregate (summed ADP over all 11
benchmarks versus summed baseline) is 23.11\%, 35.73\%, and 23.97\% for
seeds 7, 42, and 123 respectively, averaging \textbf{27.60\%}. Nine of eleven benchmarks are consistently
positive and stable; \texttt{raygentop} is the only persistent failure
($[-2.2, -1.7]\%$ across all seeds). \texttt{or1200} and \texttt{boundtop}
are positive but highly seed-sensitive, spanning 28--29 points each and
driving most of the aggregate variance. In-pool performance is considerably
more seed-sensitive than zero-shot, which stays within $\sim$12 points across
all six held-out benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}): early
convergence on most training benchmarks leaves fewer episodes for
slow-to-converge ones like \texttt{or1200}, so which seed handles them well
varies run to run.

\subsection{GA Comparison}
\label{sec:results-ga}

For a fair point of comparison, we implement a GA under the same oracle,
ADP metric, and baseline on \texttt{softmax}: roulette-wheel selection,
single-point crossover, per-gene mutation over H-block coordinates and
aspect ratio, population 8, capped at 500 generations with patience 50.
The GA and the policy search in fundamentally different ways, a GA
generation is not an RL episode, so we compare the one quantity that is
directly comparable between them: the best layout each one finds. The
GA's best reaches \textbf{\GAred\%} ADP reduction. Zero-shot, the policy
already achieves \textbf{5.39--10.77\%} across seeds (7, 42, 123) with no
search at all; fine-tuned on \texttt{softmax} from seed 42, it reaches
\textbf{14.82\%}, matching the GA's best.

The GA pays its search cost again from scratch for every new design.
The trained policy pays its cost once and reuses it: zero-shot lands a
positive result before any \texttt{softmax}-specific search begins, and
the advantage compounds with the number of designs the same checkpoint
is later applied to.

Figure~\ref{fig:layout} shows the physical saving on \texttt{softmax}. The
traditional fabric provisions BRAM columns the netlist never uses; the policy
omits them and selects an aspect ratio under which VTR auto-sizes the logic
to a $22\%$ smaller array, the dominant ADP term. The policy never moves
a CLB; it removes idle columns and reshapes the fabric, and the tighter
routing is VTR's.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Generalization}
\label{sec:results-zeroshot}

Figure~\ref{fig:seedvar} (lower group) reports ADP reduction on six held-out
benchmarks across the same three seeds, with zero benchmark-specific training.
All six fit within the checkpoint's fixed dimensions. Only two
(\texttt{softmax}, \texttt{reduction\_layer}) were used to choose those
dimensions; the other four were sized in by benchmarks elsewhere in the
sizing superset, a harder test of generalization.
Four of the six are larger than anything in the training
pool (up to $2{,}304$ tiles vs.\ $676$), making this size extrapolation, not
interpolation. Every benchmark is positive under every seed (no sign flips),
and every per-seed range is narrow ($\le12$ points), far from the
\texttt{or1200}/\texttt{boundtop} spread. The overall average is
\textbf{31.23\%}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper trains one policy across 11 circuits and compares it to a GA
under the same oracle, metric, and baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results-ga}):
the fine-tuned policy reaches a similar best ADP reduction, and zero-shot
gives a positive result with no per-design search. The same policy applies
zero-shot to six held-out benchmarks, four larger than anything in
training (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}).

These results rest on three seeds and one deterministic rollout per
zero-shot benchmark, with in-pool fitting showing more seed sensitivity
than zero-shot transfer within that sample (Section~\ref{sec:results-inpool}).
A few directions follow naturally: a systematic stochastic-rollout
evaluation; extending the action space, currently placement and aspect
ratio only, to co-evolve CLB LUT size and block sizing~\cite{nsgaii}; and
running on the full GOLDS/NSGA-II benchmark set for direct comparison.
